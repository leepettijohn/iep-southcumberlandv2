A Letter to Our Community · 2026

Beersheba Springs & Altamont

What residents shared this year
Dear Residents and Leaders of Beersheba Springs and Altamont,

Your residents have made something unmistakably clear: this is a place people deeply believe in. Across the data, one of the most powerful shifts from last year to this year is the substantial rise in Commitment, even as Vision and Leadership remained relatively stable. That tells an extraordinary story. Residents' emotional connection, loyalty, and desire to remain rooted in this community are growing faster than the systems that guide the future. In short, belief in place is rising.

At the same time, the challenges are real. Economic confidence remains negative, with employment opportunities continuing to weigh heavily on families and long-term stability. Housing, childcare, physical healthcare, and access to stronger wage pathways remain urgent priorities. These are not simply service gaps — they are the foundational systems that determine whether young families stay, whether seniors can age with dignity, and whether future generations can imagine building their lives here.

Yet what gives this community uncommon strength is that hope has not faded. When residents look ahead, every measure of future economic confidence lifts above where today stands — most strikingly in employment, where the gap between current hardship and future possibility is wide. Residents are signaling that they can see a path forward and they are asking for structures that match the commitment they already feel: clearer pathways for leadership, visible plans for the future, stronger support for working households, and meaningful opportunities for young people to remain connected to the life of the town.

The rise in Commitment is perhaps the clearest invitation in this report. People are ready. They care more deeply than ever. The opportunity now is to meet that growing emotional energy with action — transforming belonging into ownership, ownership into leadership, and leadership into measurable progress. By building on trust, preserving the identity that makes this place special, and investing in the systems families need most, Beersheba Springs - Altamont can convert today's loyalty into tomorrow's renewal.

We are honored to support your journey and believe the next chapter for this community can be one of rooted resilience, practical progress, and renewed confidence in the future.

Subject 1 · Vibrant Place

Housing

Housing was the single strongest signal in this year's survey. More residents named affordable housing as a top program priority than any other item — and it surfaced again in residents' biggest dreams, in what they would fund with modest dollars, and in the businesses they say the community is missing. The picture is consistent: stable homes are the foundation residents say everything else depends on.

  • 39% of residents named affordable housing the top program priority — the highest-ranked priority in the survey. Programs · Healthy
  • Residents chose Affordable Housing as the top item in the unconstrained "if money were no object" project list. Unconstrained
  • Affordable & Family Housing appears again in the realistic $15K-per-year project list — meaning residents would back it with limited dollars too. $15K Panel
  • Affordable Housing ranks among the top 5 businesses residents say the community needs. Businesses
  • 26% named blight one of the metrics they most want tracked over the next three years — a related signal of housing-quality concern. Metrics · Place
Subject 2 · Healthy Residents & Engaged Residents

Childcare & Youth/Family

Residents repeatedly described family stability as the system that holds everything else together. Childcare ranks at the top of the program list, youth and family programs appear in both project panels, and food insecurity — which families feel most acutely — was the #1 metric residents asked be tracked. Together these signals point to a community wanting to make it easier for working parents to stay, work, and raise kids here.

  • 36% of residents named childcare a top program priority — tied for second-highest in the survey. Programs · Healthy
  • Youth & Family Programs were named in residents' unconstrained project vision — building childcare, youth activities, and safe gathering spaces. Unconstrained
  • Youth Recreation & Engagement ranks first in the realistic $15K-per-year project list. $15K Panel
  • Childcare ranks #3 among the businesses residents say the community needs. Businesses
  • 32% named food insecurity the #1 metric to track — the strongest signal of family-stress pressure in the dataset. Metrics · Healthy
Subject 3 · Resilient Economy

Workforce & Economic Mobility

Residents are clear-eyed about how hard the local economy feels right now — and equally clear about what could change it. Workforce training and certifications tied as a top program priority, and the metrics residents most want tracked center on income and the kinds of businesses that pay better wages. The picture beneath these numbers is a community asking for ladders, not handouts: pathways to better jobs that exist close enough to home that families don't have to leave.

  • 36% of residents named workforce training and certifications a top program priority — tied for second-highest. Programs · Engaged
  • Workforce & Life Skills Training appears in the $15K-per-year project list — residents would invest modest dollars here. $15K Panel
  • 29% named average household income as a metric to track; 21% named above-average wage businesses. Metrics · Economy
  • Factories / Jobs appears in residents' top 10 businesses-needed list — a signal that better wage opportunities are wanted close to home. Businesses
  • 33% named startup funding a top priority — a related signal of residents wanting routes into ownership and entrepreneurship. Programs · Economy
Notable Mentions

Just below the top three

These subjects didn't lead the rankings but show real and consistent support across multiple parts of the survey. Worth keeping on the table as the community moves forward.

Healthcare

Healthcare ties for second among program priorities and surfaces strongly in residents' project visions — yet it didn't rise to a top-3 subject because it lacks a matching metric in the most-requested list. Still, the depth of evidence is real, and a coordinated healthcare expansion would touch many of the same families being held up by housing and childcare gaps.

  • 36% named physical healthcare a top program priority. Programs · Healthy
  • Healthcare Expansion appears in the unconstrained project list; Community Wellness Center appears in the $15K-per-year list. Both Panels
  • Healthcare Services ranks #2 among businesses residents say the community needs. Businesses

Senior Support & Aging in Place

Senior services don't appear in the priority rankings — that survey question doesn't include them as an option — but they show up across residents' open-ended visions. Both unconstrained and modest-funding panels name dedicated support for older residents, and it surfaces in the businesses-needed list too.

  • Senior Support Services appears in residents' unconstrained project list — in-home help, meals, transportation, and wellness. Unconstrained
  • Senior Services ranks among the top 10 businesses residents say the community needs. Businesses
  • Retired residents (n=12) prioritize beautification, student engagement, and housing access — distinct from working residents. Crosstabs

Beautification & Tourism

Less prominent than the family-stability subjects, but a recurring chord — especially among newer residents and renters. It speaks to a community that wants to feel proud of its public spaces and recognized for its mountain identity.

  • Community Beautification & Tourism appears in the $15K-per-year project list. $15K Panel
  • Newer residents and renters (smaller subgroups) name blight and beautification as top concerns. Crosstabs
  • 26% named blight as one of the metrics they most want tracked. Metrics · Place

Every project residents imagined

If money were no object: the unconstrained project

  • Affordable Housing — Expand low-income and workforce housing to reduce instability and strengthen long-term family roots
  • Senior Support Services — Provide in-home help, meals, transportation, and wellness assistance to improve quality of life
  • Youth & Family Programs — Build childcare, youth activities, and safe gathering spaces that strengthen family connection
  • Healthcare Expansion — Grow clinic services including mental health and physical therapy to keep care close to home
  • Economic & Community Development — Repurpose historic buildings for job training, light manufacturing, and civic space

With $15K a year for three years

  • Youth Recreation & Engagement — Expand parks, play spaces, internships, and hobbies to reduce risky behaviors
  • Community Wellness Center — Create a shared space for healthcare, mental health, youth, internet, and events
  • Workforce & Life Skills Training — Build job readiness, education, and resilience skills to grow confidence and income
  • Affordable & Family Housing — Support stable housing for single parents and vulnerable families
  • Community Beautification & Tourism — Improve parks, streets, and destination spaces to build pride and attract visitors

How residents already serve

  • Neighbor Support — Checking on neighbors and helping with immediate daily needs
  • Food Assistance — Delivering meals and food during hardship, loss, or fundraisers
  • Volunteer Visitation — Visiting residents who need companionship or practical help
  • Emergency Response Support — Assisting fire and rescue squad efforts when needed
  • Civic & Faith Leadership — Serving as commissioner, church member, business owner, or voice for the community

How residents want to serve next

  • Civic Leadership & Advocacy — Serving in office, speaking up, and helping guide better local decisions
  • Volunteer Project Support — Helping with community projects, clean-up, and practical needs as time allows
  • Youth Mentorship & Responsibility — Teaching children and grandchildren stewardship, service, and community pride
  • Clinic & Community Cause Support — Volunteering for local healthcare and supporting important shared causes
  • Faith & Community Encouragement — Offering prayer, church leadership, and encouragement for leaders and residents

1 · Methodology

This dossier reports findings from the 2026 Beersheba Springs - Altamont community survey, fielded as part of the South Cumberland regional benchmark. Responses were collected via Google Form and analyzed under the IEP composite-confidence framework.

Composite confidence scoring

For each pillar (Commitment, Vision, Leadership), the score is the mean of all item-level means on a 1–7 scale, divided by 7, expressed as a percentage. Sub-pillar scores apply the same formula scoped to the items within each sub-pillar.

Economic perception scoring

Each economic question maps a five-rung verbal scale to numeric values (best = +1.0, mid = 0.0, worst = −1.0) using longest-prefix matching. The reported score is the mean × 100. Negative values indicate net pessimism.

Priority parsing

Multi-select priority responses were parsed by splitting on category boundaries before naive comma splitting, since item text contains internal commas. Percentages use the count of non-null responses to each question as the denominator.

Benchmark

The "Regional" benchmark column is the mean across all areas in the 2026 South Cumberland dataset. Year-over-year deltas reference values stored in the cycle's yoy-master.xlsx workbook, filtered to Beersheba Springs - Altamont.

Subgroup sizes under 10 are flagged as directional rather than statistical throughout this dossier.
